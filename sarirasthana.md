# Caraka Samhita — Śārīrasthāna (Anatomy / Embryology / The Self)

Extracted from *Caraka Samhita*, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma (Chaukhambha
Orientalia, 2014).


## The Nature of the Self (Ātman / Puruṣa)

> "There can not be light, darkness, truth, falsehood, scripture, auspicious
> and inauspicious actions if there be not the active and intelligent person.
> There would be no substratum, happiness, misery, going and coming, speech,
> understanding, treatises, life and death, knowledge and emancipation if the
> person were not there. That is why the person is recognised as the cause by
> the experts in causation."
> — C.Śā. 1.39–41

> "The personal self is known as the cause from all the sources of valid
> knowledge and also all the scriptures from which the knowables are known."
> — C.Śā. 1.45

> "The self which is beginningless is eternal but that which is a product of a
> cause is contrary. One which is not caused is ever-existing and eternal while
> that which is caused is otherwise."
> — C.Śā. 1.59

> "The self, the knower of the body, eternal, omnipresent and indestructible is
> the unmanifest."
> — C.Śā. 1.61

> "The self possesses knowledge which comes forth on conjunction with
> instruments and does not come forth in the condition of their impurity or in
> absence of their contact. As there is no vision in a dirty mirror or muddy
> water even though one sees through, there is no perception if the mind etc.
> are damaged."
> — C.Śā. 1.54–55

> "The self is omnipresent because he is all-pervasive and great. Hence if he
> concentrates his mind he can perceive even the hidden. That associated for
> ever with mind in accordance with the action of the body should be taken as
> pervading all species even though apparently stationed in one species."
> — C.Śā. 1.80–81

> "There is no beginning of the self and the continuity of the body. Hence,
> both being beginningless, it is impossible to say which is earlier."
> — C.Śā. 1.82

> "Only the one having knowledge can be witness not the ignorant one — that is
> why the self is said as an observer. All the entities of all the living
> beings are observed by the self."
> — C.Śā. 1.83

> "Mind is unconscious but active while the self kindles consciousness but is
> inactive. The omnipresent one becomes active only when it comes in contact
> with mind. The self is said as 'doer' because of having consciousness while
> mind, devoid of consciousness, is not taken as such inspite of his
> activeness."
> — C.Śā. 1.75–76


## Consciousness, Body, and the Relationship Between Them

> "All by his respective self leads himself to all the species attaining life
> and controlling with Prāṇa, none else is his master."
> — C.Śā. 1.77

> "Desire, aversion, happiness, misery, will, consciousness, control,
> knowledge, memory and ego — these are the signs of the self. As these signs
> are found only in living beings and not in dead ones, the sages have said
> them as the signs of the self. After His departure, the body is converted to
> a lifeless, vacant house and is said to have reached the 'fiveness' because
> of only five mahābhūtas remaining there."
> — C.Śā. 1.70–74

> "The person is equal to the universe. Whatever formed entities are found in
> the universe, they are also found in the person and vice versa. The learned
> people want to see the phenomena with this angle."
> — C.Śā. 4.13

> "In the Person pṛthivī, ap, tejas, vāyu, ākāśa and Brahman are represented
> by form, moisture, heat, vital breath, pores and inner self respectively. As
> is the magnificence of Brahman in the universe so is that of inner self in
> the Person."
> — C.Śā. 5.5

> "Seeing the entire universe in the Self and vice versa gives rise to true
> knowledge. On seeing the entire universe in his Self one realises that self
> alone is the agent of happiness and misery and none else."
> — C.Śā. 5.7


## Embryology and Formation of the Body

> "When there is cohabitation of man having undamaged sperm and woman having
> undamaged genital tract, ovum and uterus during the ṛtukāla and
> consequently the conscious self impelled by mind descends into the zygote
> situated in the uterus, the embryo is formed."
> — C.Śā. 3.3

> "The self of embryo is the internal self which is known as 'jīva' — source
> of life — and also as ever-continuing, devoid of illness, old age, death and
> decay; not subjected to incision, excision, agitation; having universal forms
> and actions, unmanifest, beginningless as well as endless and without any
> transformation. He entering into the uterus and combining with sperm and
> ovum produces himself in the form of embryo."
> — C.Śā. 3.8

> "The self alongwith subtle four bhūtas and with speed like that of mind
> transmigrates from one body to the other according to past deeds. It can not
> be seen without divine visual sense. He is omnivagant, sustains all bodies,
> performs all actions, takes all forms. He is the source of consciousness,
> transcending all senses, ever-united and closely attached."
> — C.Śā. 2.31

> "Embryo is the product of ākāśa, vāyu, tejas, ap and pṛthivī being the
> seat of consciousness. Thus, embryo is the aggregate of the five mahābhūtas
> being the seat of consciousness which is regarded as the sixth constituent of
> embryo."
> — C.Śā. 4.6

> "The aspects of the embryo caused by the self are these: birth in a
> particular species, life-span, self-knowledge, mind, sense organs,
> respiration, impulse, sustenance, characteristic physiognomy, voice and
> complexion, happiness, misery, desire, aversion, consciousness, restraint,
> intellect, memory, ego and will."
> — C.Śā. 3.10


## Rebirth, Karma, and Past Lives

> "The deed of the past life known as 'daiva' is also observed as the cause of
> diseases in particular time."
> — C.Śā. 1.116

> "There is no major action the fruit of which is not enjoyed. The diseases
> caused by the deeds of past life neutralise the therapeutic measures and
> subside only on destruction of the deeds."
> — C.Śā. 1.117

> "The deeds of the previous life are known as daiva and those of the present
> life as pauruṣa. These in unbalanced way cause diseases while in balanced
> one avert them."
> — C.Śā. 2.44

> "At the time of dissolution, the self gets detached from these enjoyable
> entities. Thus the self, subdued by rājas and tamas, gets manifested from
> the unmanifest state and then reverts to the unmanifest and thus moves
> continuously like a wheel from one state to another. Those who are attached
> excessively to duals and are engrossed by ego are subjected to origin and
> destruction but not others who are contrary to them."
> — C.Śā. 1.67–69

> "According to past deeds, form arises from the past form and mind from the
> past mind. Whatever difference is observed in physique and psyche is caused
> by rājas and tamas as well as the past deed."
> — C.Śā. 2.36

> "In case the psyche is pure, one recollects the incidents of the previous
> birth. This sort of recollective knowledge comes forth by the association of
> mind with the self on the basis of which the person is said as 'jātismara'
> — one who recollects the previous life."
> — C.Śā. 3.13


## The Nature of the Mind (Manas)

> "Derangement of intellect, restraint, and memory; advent of time and action
> and contact with unsuitable sense objects should be known as the causes of
> misery."
> — C.Śā. 1.98

> "Adherence to wrong discrimination between eternal and non-eternal as well
> as wholesome and unwholesome is known as derangement of intellect because by
> nature the intellect sees rightly."
> — C.Śā. 1.99

> "Happiness and misery arise due to contact of the self, sense organs, mind
> and the sense objects but when the mind is steadily concentrated to the self,
> both cease to exist due to non-initiation and a supernatural power comes
> forth in the person. This state is known as 'yoga' by the expert sages."
> — C.Śā. 1.138–139

> "The dirty psyche gets cleaned by these means like a mirror by oil, cloth,
> brush etc.; then the psyche, being purified, shines like the solar disc
> uncovered by any other planet, clouds, dust, smoke and fog. That psyche
> concentrated into the self with all doors closed illumines like the lamp
> situated in the lamp case, pure, stable and with pleasant light."
> — C.Śā. 5.13–15


## Philosophical Insights on Existence and Emancipation

> "All caused is source of misery, dependent and non-eternal. That is not
> concerned with the self but only a wrong notion of mine-ness arises until
> the true knowledge emerges. But hereafter with its help knowing that 'I am
> not this body,' 'this body is not mine,' the knower of truth transcends
> all."
> — C.Śā. 1.152–153

> "The supreme treatment is that which is devoid of allurement. Allurement is
> the greatest cause of misery and the abode of miseries, and renunciation of
> all allurements eliminates all miseries. As a silkworm brings forth threads
> leading to its death, the ignorant and ever-ill person creates allurement
> from the sense objects."
> — C.Śā. 1.94–96

> "Mokṣa is possible by absence of rājas and tamas, destruction of the potent
> past deeds and detachment from all the sources of conjunction. It is also
> said as absence of rebirth."
> — C.Śā. 1.142

> "The power of recollection of the reality is the only way to emancipation as
> shown by the emancipated ones, by which one having gone never returns."
> — C.Śā. 1.150–151

> "Disinclination is apavarga. This is also known as the Supreme, Highest
> bliss, identity with the unchangeable Brahman and freedom from bondage."
> — C.Śā. 5.11

> "Mokṣa or Śānti is known by the synonyms — vipāpa, virajas, śānta, para,
> akṣara, avyaya, amṛta, Brahman and nirvāṇa."
> — C.Śā. 5.23

> "The pure and true knowledge comes forth from the pure mind by which the
> tamas, very strong and consisting of great ignorance, is dispelled; one
> becomes free from desires by knowing the nature of all beings, yoga is
> accomplished and sāṃkhya is attained."
> — C.Śā. 5.16

> "Thus the body having many parts if seen synthetically leads to attachment
> and viewed analytically leads to emancipation. The principal one which is
> detached retires after all existent entities cease to exist."
> — C.Śā. 7.18
