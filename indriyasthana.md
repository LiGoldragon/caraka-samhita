# Caraka Samhita — Indriyasthāna (Prognosis / Signs of Death)

Extracted from *Caraka Samhita*, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma (Chaukhambha
Orientalia, 2014).


## The Nature of Fatal Signs (Ariṣṭa)

> "As flower is manifestation preceding the would-be fruit, the fatal signs
> known as 'ariṣṭa' precede death."
> — C.In. 2.3

> "Sometimes a flower may not be followed by a fruit or a fruit may appear
> without a preceding flower — but the arisen fatal signs do not faint away
> without leading to death; on the other hand, there is no death which is not
> preceded by the fatal signs."
> — C.In. 2.4–5

> "The signs produced by the dosas having transcended the remedial measures and
> which pervade the entire body are known as 'ariṣṭa' (fatal signs)."
> — C.In. 11.29

> "These signs in human beings arise at the approach of death. The physician
> should observe these and other similar signs. They all are observed but are
> not necessarily found in one person approaching death. Hence all the signs
> should be known."
> — C.In. 9.23–24


## The Shadow, Complexion, and Aura of the Dying

> "If there is any deformity observed in body parts in the shadow of the person
> in moonlight, sunlight, light of a lamp, water or mirror, he should be taken
> as ghost."
> — C.In. 7.4

> "Person projecting shadow as severed, torn, confused, diminished, exceeded,
> and other such despicable shadows should be taken as about to die if they are
> not caused by specific morbidity."
> — C.In. 7.5–6

> "If a person, even healthy, has his image distorted in respect of shape,
> measurement, colour and lustre, he should be considered as ghost."
> — C.In. 7.7

> "Chaya invades complexion while 'bha' illumines the same. Chaya is observed
> from nearby while bha shines from a distance. There is none who is devoid of
> chaya and prabha; the different features of chaya and prabha arisen in
> certain times indicate the auspiciousness or otherwise for the person."
> — C.In. 7.16–17

> "Normal complexion in one half and abnormal one in the other half of the
> body — demarcated clearly as left and right, front and back, upper and
> lower — should be known as ariṣṭa of the patient."
> — C.In. 1.10

> "Black moles, moles, freckles and various streaks appear suddenly on the
> face of a patient who is about to expire."
> — C.In. 1.22

> "The patient whose lips are blue like ripe jamuna fruits should be regarded
> as one devoid of life-span and about to die."
> — C.In. 1.25


## The Deterioration of the Senses

> "The perception pertaining to the sense organs which is abnormal and arisen
> without an apparent cause is indicative of death."
> — C.In. 4.5

> "The patient who sees the sky as solidified like earth or the earth like the
> sky, both in contradiction, attains death."
> — C.In. 4.7

> "When one is going to give up his existence, he sees brilliant objects as
> devoid of brilliance, or non-brilliant objects as brilliant ones, or other
> objects devoid of their normal characters. The persons, at the end of life,
> see the objects as deformed, of different colour, in different number without
> an apparent cause. If one sees the non-visible objects or does not see the
> visible ones — both types of persons die soon."
> — C.In. 4.13–18

> "One who hears sound when there is none or does not perceive the real one —
> both these types of person should be taken as almost dead. One who does not
> hear the heat-sound after closing the ears with fingers should be known as
> devoid of life-span and should be discarded."
> — C.In. 4.19–20

> "Super-sensory perception without austere penance or methodical concentration
> of mind indicates death. If one perceives the senses rightly in spite of
> incapability of the sense organs, it indicates death."
> — C.In. 4.24–25

> "At the approaching death, complexion, voice, power of digestion, strength
> of speech, sense organs and mind — all these diminish, and sleep comes ever
> or never."
> — C.In. 11.24


## The Limits of Medicine and the Physician's Duty

> "The four legs of treatment endowed with effective properties, even if
> combined together, become useless in case of the patients having spent up
> their life-span — because there can be no effect without a cause."
> — C.In. 11.27

> "If a remedy well-known, well-tried and administered properly does not
> succeed, the case may be considered as incurable."
> — C.In. 12.7

> "The physician, though observing the signs of death, should not disclose the
> approaching death without having been requested for. Even on request, he
> should not express it if it is liable to cause the patient's death or
> affliction to somebody else. The experienced physician, without expressing
> the patient's death, should not be inclined to treat him after observing the
> fatal signs."
> — C.In. 12.62–64

> "One who knows these signs well, knows the life as well as the death of the
> mortals."
> — C.In. 10.21


## Philosophical Reflections on Death and the Departing Self

> "Now I will describe the symptoms and change in condition indicating
> departure of the self who, having lived in the body for the maximum period,
> prepares to migrate out of the body after relinquishing the beloved vital
> breath, giving up the beautiful abode and entering into the final darkness —
> when all the systems and organs are disintegrated."
> — C.In. 12.43

> "When diseases aggravate severely and mental strength deteriorates, the owner
> of the body leaves his abode known as 'deha'."
> — C.In. 11.23

> "Inclination, conduct, memory, sacrifice, intellect and strength — these
> six entities go away from the patient who is to die within six months."
> — C.In. 11.7

> "The patients who develop dislike for physician, medicines, food, preceptor
> and friends should be known as having moved under control of the God of
> death. In these persons the disease advances while the medicament becomes
> ineffective."
> — C.In. 11.25

> "A patient having his mind covered with great tamas does not perceive even
> though looking at their kins or attendants who are calling him from nearby."
> — C.In. 11.21


## Bodily Signs of Imminent Death

> "If one sees the sun in night, or the moon in day, or smoke arisen in
> absence of fire, or lustreless fire in night — he is about to die."
> — C.In. 4.15

> "The person expressing his dislike for all those entities which were very
> much liked by him previously, and who is deteriorating, should be taken as
> about to die."
> — C.In. 8.21

> "The patient who is about to die has his neck unable to keep the head on,
> his back unable to take up the weight of his body, and his jaws unable to
> bear the morsel of the mouth."
> — C.In. 8.22

> "Teeth as anointed with mud, face as if powdered, body parts having cold
> clammy perspiration — these indicate the patient's sudden death."
> — C.In. 10.19

> "The patient who searches for his fingers and hands putting them before his
> eyes and smiles while having his eyes directed upwards and unblinking should
> be taken as blinded by death. The patient who, being confused, searches for
> something non-existing in bed, seat, body parts, wooden furniture or wall
> should be taken as impelled by time."
> — C.In. 11.18–19

> "If a patient takes favourite and nutritious food but is constantly reduced
> in strength and complexion, he does not survive."
> — C.In. 7.23

> "The wise physician should observe attentively these signs in persons who are
> about to die — because some of them after arising disappear in a moment, and
> no sign goes without its consequence."
> — C.In. 8.27


## Smell, Taste, and the Body's Final Transformations

> "The patient who gets smell — desirable or undesirable — in the body
> suddenly and without an apparent cause does not survive till the end of the
> year."
> — C.In. 2.15

> "Flies, lice, stinging insects and mosquitos get away from the body with the
> unpleasant taste of a person who is about to die. On the other hand, flies
> are attracted constantly to the exceedingly sweet body of a patient who is
> ripened enough for the time, even after bath and application of cosmetics."
> — C.In. 2.19–21
