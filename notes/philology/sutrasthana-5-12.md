# Sūtrasthāna 5.12 — the *saindhavān lavaṇaṃ ghṛtam* question

## Why this verse matters

Caraka 5.12 is widely cited as the classical Ayurvedic warrant for including *saindhava* (rock salt) in the daily diet. The modern popular-wellness industry uses this verse — often without attribution — to justify the daily use of "pink Himalayan salt" and other variants of chloride of sodium as an Ayurvedic practice.

But the received Sanskrit of this verse has **three independent anomalies** that modern English translators smooth over without acknowledgment. Each anomaly, taken seriously, weakens the case for treating the verse as a prescription for daily rock salt. Taken together, they suggest that the "daily rock salt" reading is the translator's choice, not what the Sanskrit unambiguously says.

This note records the state of that question as of 2026-04-23.

## The received Sanskrit

From the scan of page 074 of the Priya Vrat Sharma 2014 Chaukhambha edition ([`.ocr_pages/page-074.png`](../../.ocr_pages/page-074.png), captured in [`../../pages/074.txt`](../../pages/074.txt) with the one OCR correction logged in [`../ocr-corrections.md`](../ocr-corrections.md)):

## Anomaly 1 — *saindhavān* is plural and does not agree with *lavaṇaṃ*

### What Sharma's translation asserts

> "One should take as daily routine *ṣaṣṭika*, *śāli* (rice), *mudga* (pulse), **saindhava (rock salt)**, *āmalaka* (fruits), barley, rain water, milk, ghee and honey."
> — *Caraka Saṃhitā*, Sūtrasthāna 5.12, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma

Sharma reads *saindhavān lavaṇaṃ* as a compound: *saindhava-lavaṇa* = "rock salt," a single item.

### Why that reading is imposed rather than read out of the text

If *saindhava* were functioning as an adjective modifying *lavaṇa*, classical Sanskrit grammar would require agreement in gender, number, and case. The expected form would be:

> *saindhavaṃ lavaṇaṃ* — both accusative singular neuter ✓

What the received text gives is:

> *saindhavān* — accusative **plural masculine** (-ān ending)
> *lavaṇaṃ* — accusative **singular neuter** (-aṃ ending)

These do not agree. They are two different grammatical targets. If the verse meant "rock salt" as one item, the scribe or poet had the cleaner form *saindhavaṃ lavaṇaṃ* readily available and used the un-agreeing form instead.

### Metrical check

Both readings scan in anuṣṭubh meter:

- *sain-dha-vān-la-va-ṇaṃ-ghṛ-tam* = 8 syllables ✓
- *sain-dha-vaṃ-la-va-ṇaṃ-ghṛ-tam* = 8 syllables ✓

Meter is indifferent. The choice is genuinely textual.

### What *saindhavān* (acc. pl.) could mean if read as a separate item

*Saindhava* has multiple classical senses:

1. **Rock salt** — the specialized Ayurvedic meaning; almost always travels as a compound *saindhava-lavaṇa* or as a singular *saindhavam*.
2. **A horse** — *saindhava* is the standard classical word for a horse (from Sindh, the horse-breeding region). Semantically inappropriate in a dinacaryā food list, but morphologically plausible for *saindhavān* (acc. pl.).
3. **Things / products / inhabitants of Sindh** — a broader regional designation (trade goods, for example).
4. **A kind of sea fish** — attested in some lexicographic sources, especially for fish from the Sindhu estuary. *Saindhavān* (acc. pl.) would fit this reading semantically in a diet list.

Reading **4** is unlikely to be what modern traditional Ayurvedic commentators have accepted, because it would contradict the yogic *apathya* list (HYP 1.59) which forbids fish (*matsya*) outright. But it is morphologically available.

### Possible explanations for the received reading

1. **Scribal corruption:** an original *saindhavaṃ lavaṇaṃ* got corrupted in the manuscript tradition to *saindhavān lavaṇaṃ*; commentators post hoc harmonized the reading as "rock salt."
2. **Nasal-sandhi artifact:** *saindhavaṃ lavaṇaṃ* could be orally realized as *saindhavāṃl̐lavaṇaṃ* through nasal-assimilation sandhi and then written as *saindhavān lavaṇaṃ* by a scribe unfamiliar with the sandhi.
3. **Archaic morphology:** the verse is old and the *saindhavān* form reflects a dialectal/archaic accusative pattern that later grammarians regularized.
4. **Genuinely separate items:** the verse is listing *saindhavān* (something plural — perhaps regional products) and *lavaṇaṃ* (salt as a *rasa*, or salt generically) as two distinct items, and later commentators silently merged them.

**Without access to Cakrapāṇidatta's *Āyurveda Dīpikā* commentary, none of these can be confirmed or ruled out.** The commentary is the canonical resolver for this kind of anomaly. See [`../translation-sources.md`](../translation-sources.md) for the state of our efforts to acquire it.

## Anomaly 2 — *dhānyaṃ* is not "rain water"

Sharma renders *dhānyaṃ* (clearly "grain" in classical Sanskrit) as "rain water." This is puzzling on its face.

Two possibilities:

1. Sharma is translating from an unmarked commentary gloss. Some commentators read *dhānya* in this context as shorthand for *dhānyāmla* (sour fermented rice-water) or *dhānya-udaka* (rice-water). Neither is literally "rain water," but both involve water and grain, and a loose translation could collapse into "rain water."
2. Sharma's manuscript had a variant reading. Some editions do include *āntarīkṣa-jala* ("sky-water" = rainwater) among daily foods elsewhere in Caraka; a scribe could have substituted that reading into this verse.

A second English translator also reads "rain water" — see below. The convergence suggests a shared commentarial tradition, not an isolated Sharma mistake.

## Anomaly 3 — *ghṛtam* appears twice

The verse lists *ghṛtam* (ghee) in pāda 1 and again as *ghṛtaṃ* in pāda 2. Both are accusative singular neuter. The meter scans cleanly both times.

Sharma's English lists ghee once. So does the wisdomlib rendering (see below). Both translators silently remove the doubling.

Possibilities:

- The doubling is rhetorical emphasis: ghee is the central dietary fat of Ayurveda and the verse names it twice deliberately.
- The doubling is scribal: one *ghṛtam* is an interpolation that both translators (with editorial intuition) excised.
- The received text has corrupted an originally different word in one of the two positions. For example, pāda 2's *ghṛtaṃ* could have replaced some other neuter singular accusative (e.g., *dadhi* — curds, *takram* — buttermilk) in transmission.

Unresolved without commentary.

## Second-source check: wisdomlib.org

The wisdomlib English translation of Sū. 5.12 (checked 2026-04-23, via WebFetch of their Sūtrasthāna chapter 5 page):

> "Shashtika rice, shali rice, green gram, rock-salt, emblic myrobalan, barley, rain water, milk, ghee, **flesh of jangala animals**, and honey may be habitually taken."

**New finding.** wisdomlib's list includes ***flesh of jangala animals*** (*jāṅgala-māṃsa*, the meat of wild game from dry/forested regions) — an item **absent** from Sharma's translation. The two translations otherwise agree closely (including the spurious "rain water" for *dhānyaṃ*).

Interpretation: there is real **manuscript variance** in Sū. 5.12. At least one textual lineage has *jāṅgala-māṃsa* among the daily-routine foods; the Chaukhambha edition does not. The Sanskrit as we have it (11 accusative targets, assuming *ghṛtam* appears twice deliberately) does not contain an obvious place where *jāṅgala-māṃsa* was dropped or inserted — which suggests the variance is at the level of entire hemistichs, not single words.

## Current state and research priorities

### What has been confirmed

- The received Sanskrit (in the Priya Vrat Sharma 2014 edition) reads: *ṣaṣṭikā-śāli-mudgāṃś ca saindhavān lavaṇaṃ ghṛtam | āmalakāni dhānyaṃ ca yavān kṣīraṃ ghṛtaṃ madhu ||12||*
- One OCR typo (*ānalakāni* → *āmalakāni*) has been corrected and logged.
- Sharma's English translation makes translator-choices in three places (compound-formation of *saindhavān lavaṇaṃ*, rendering of *dhānyaṃ*, handling of doubled *ghṛtam*) that are not explicitly justified against the Sanskrit.
- A second English rendering (wisdomlib) confirms two of those choices and adds an eleventh item (*jāṅgala-māṃsa*) absent from Sharma — evidencing real manuscript variance.

### What remains unresolved

- Whether the canonical traditional commentary (Cakrapāṇidatta) reads *saindhavān lavaṇaṃ* as a compound, as two separate items, or as a scribal corruption.
- Whether the Sharma/Dash 7-volume commentary-based edition preserves the same received Sanskrit.
- Whether the critical editions (Trikamji; Yogendranath Sen; more recent critical work) show manuscript variants that would resolve the grammatical issue.
- What the original (or proto-critical) reading was.

### Priority actions

1. **Acquire Cakrapāṇi's commentary.** Either via the 1922 Nirnaya Sagar PDF on Internet Archive (733 MB, needs Devanagari OCR) or the Sharma/Dash printed 7-volume set. See [`../translation-sources.md`](../translation-sources.md).
2. **Check Trikamji critical edition** for manuscript variants on this verse.
3. **Check CCRAS online edition** for a third English rendering.

## Consequences for downstream articles

Until Anomaly 1 is resolved:

- Any article in [`~/git/TheBookOfSol/`](../../../TheBookOfSol/) that cites Sū. 5.12 should acknowledge that the "daily rock salt" reading is Sharma's interpretive choice, not unambiguously what the Sanskrit says.
- The project's independent argument against dietary chloride of sodium (see *Chloridism*, *The Chloride Indictment*, *Apathya*) does not depend on how Sū. 5.12 is read. The chemistry of chloride of sodium is the same regardless of whether Caraka "admitted" it in small quantity. *Saindhava* marketing relies specifically on this verse to legitimate modern pink-salt habits; the verse is philologically too ambiguous to carry that legitimation.
