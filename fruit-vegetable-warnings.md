# Caraka Saṃhitā: Warnings on Fruits and Vegetables

Extracted from *Caraka Saṃhitā*, trans. Priya Vrat Sharma (Chaukhambha
Orientalia, 2014). All references are to Sūtrasthāna (C.Su.) chapters 26-27.


## General Principles (C.Su. 27.3-4)

Food and drinks "produce energy in mind, constitution of dhātus, strength,
complexion and clarity of sense organs, **if properly taken, otherwise they
become harmful.**"

"Sour substance is **mostly pitta-aggravating** except pomegranates and āmalaka.
Sweet is **mostly kapha-aggravating** except honey and old śāli, ṣāṣṭika rice,
barley and wheat. Bitter is **mostly vāta-aggravating and non-aphrodisiac** except
the tip of vetra, guḍūcī and paṭola leaves. Pungent is **mostly vāta-aggravating
and non-aphrodisiac** except long pepper and ginger."

Most fruits are sweet or sour. By this classification, they are inherently
either kapha-aggravating or pitta-aggravating, and none are listed among the
exceptions.


## Vegetables (C.Su. 27.88-124)

### Vegetables that aggravate doṣas

- **Vegetable of tila, vetasa and eraṇḍa**: "vāta-aggravating, pungent-bitter-sour
  and purgative."
- **Vegetable of kusumbha**: "rough, sour, hot, alleviates kapha and **aggravates
  pitta**."
- **Trapusa and ervāruka** (cucumber family): "sweet, **heavy, distending**, cold,
  relishing and rough." Trapusa is "excessively diuretic."
- **Alābu** (bottle gourd): "purgative, rough, cold and **heavy**."
- **Keluta, kadamba, nandīmasaka and ainduka**: "non-slimy, **heavy, cold and
  channel-blocking**."
- **Lotus stalks, roots, krauñcadana, kaseruka, śṛṅgāṭaka and aṅkalodya**:
  "**heavy, distending and cold**."
- **Stalks of kumuda and utpala** (water lily), with flowers and fruits: "cold,
  sweet, astringent and **aggravate kapha and vāta**."
- **Vegetable of mustard**: "**aggravates three doṣas**, is constipating and
  antidiuretic."
- **Various mushrooms** (except sarpacchatraka): "cold, causing rhinitis, sweet
  and **heavy**."

### General warning on legume-based and heavy vegetables

"All legumes, phañjī, cillī, kuṭumbaka, all types of ālūka with leaves ... —
these vegetables are **heavy, rough and often digest with distension**. They are
madhura, śīta vīrya and purgative. **They should be boiled, the juice be
expressed out and then added with some fat before intake.**"

This instruction — boil, press out the juice, add fat — is the opposite of
raw consumption.


## Fruits (C.Su. 27.125-165)

### Fruits described as heavy, distending, or doṣa-aggravating

- **Āmrāta** (wild mango): "sweet, bulk-promoting, tonic, saturating, **heavy**,
  slightly unctuous, **kapha-aggravating**, cold, aphrodisiac and **digests with
  distension**."
- **Bhavya**: "sweet, sour, astringent, **distending, heavy**, cold, **aggravating
  pitta and kapha**, constipating."
- **Sour varieties of parūṣaka, grapes, jujube, āruka, small jujube and
  nikuca**: "**aggravate pitta and kapha**."
- **Bilwa (bael)**: "when ripe, is **digested with difficulty, causes doṣas and
  flatus** with foul smell."
- **Young fruits of āmra (mango)**: "cause **internal haemorrhage**." Mature ones
  "**aggravate pitta**."
- **Fruits of jambu (java plum)**: "predominantly astringent-sweet, **heavy,
  distending**, cold, alleviate kapha-pitta, are constipating and **aggravate
  vāta**."
- **Fully ripe panasa (jackfruit), moca (plantain) and rājadana**: "sweet,
  slightly astringent, unctuous, cold and **heavy**."
- **Fruit of śleṣmātaka**: "**aggravates kapha**, is sweet, cold and **heavy**."
- **Ankola fruit**: "**aggravates kapha**, is **heavy, distending and diminishes
  agni**."
- **Sour āmrātaka, dantaśāṭha, karamardaka, and airāvata**: "cause **internal
  haemorrhage**."
- **Parpaṭakī fruit**: "**aggravates vāta** and alleviates kapha and pitta."
- **Akṣikī fruit**: "sour and **aggravates vāta**."
- **Fruits of aśvattha, udumbara, plakṣa, nyagrodha** (fig family): "astringent-
  sweet, sour, **aggravate vāta** and are **heavy**."
- **Vatāma, abhiṣuka, akṣoṭa, mukulaka, nikocaka and urumāṇa** (nuts): "**heavy**,
  hot, unctuous, sweet ... **aggravate kapha and pitta**."

### Fruits with fewer warnings

- **Grapes**: alleviate thirst, burning, fever, vāta-pitta. (One of the few
  fruits praised without heavy qualification.)
- **Āmalaka**: "all rasas except lavana ... excellent alleviator of kapha and
  pitta." (The great exception.)
- **Dāḍima (pomegranate)**: sour-sweet variety "alleviates vāta ... non-
  conflicting with kapha and pitta." But the "only sour and rough" variety
  "**aggravates pitta and vāta**."


## Incompatible Combinations Involving Fruits (C.Su. 26.84)

"Āmra, āmrātaka, matuluṅga, nikuca, karamarda, moca, dantaśāṭha, badara,
kośāmra, bhavya, jambū, nārikela, dāḍima, āmalaka — **these fruits and similar
other substances, all sour liquids or non-liquids are antagonistic to milk.**"


## Summary of the Pattern

The Caraka Saṃhitā does not treat fruits and vegetables as inherently
wholesome categories. It evaluates each substance individually by rasa, vīrya,
vipāka, and prabhāva. The recurring warnings are:

1. **Heaviness** (guru): Most fruits and many vegetables are classified as heavy,
   meaning they burden agni and slow digestion.

2. **Distension** (ādhmāna): Many fruits and tuber vegetables are explicitly
   described as "distending" — producing gas, bloating, and obstructed vāyu.

3. **Channel-blocking** (srotarodha): Several vegetables are described as
   channel-blocking, the direct opposite of the free-flowing state required
   for health.

4. **Doṣa aggravation**: The majority of fruits aggravate at least one doṣa.
   Sweet fruits aggravate kapha. Sour fruits aggravate pitta. Astringent fruits
   aggravate vāta. Very few are described as "alleviating three doṣas."

5. **Preparation is mandatory**: Heavy vegetables must be "boiled, the juice
   expressed out, and added with fat." Raw consumption is not contemplated as
   a norm; the text assumes cooking, pressing, and the addition of ghee.

6. **Incompatibility**: Many fruits are listed as viruddha (antagonistic) when
   combined with milk, honey, or other substances — indicating they are not
   benign inputs that can be combined freely.

The text's highest praise is consistently reserved for **grains** (śāli rice,
ṣāṣṭika rice, barley, wheat — "sweet, cold, light, alleviates three doṣas"),
**ghee** ("the best among uncting substances"), and **milk** ("vitalises"). Fruits
and vegetables receive qualified, conditional, often cautionary descriptions.
They are permitted, sometimes useful, frequently heavy, and never elevated to
the status of primary nourishment.
